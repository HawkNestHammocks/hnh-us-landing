Chapter 1

I Thought I Knew BetterOr: the stubbornness of a 20-year tent camper

I'm going to be honest with you. When my buddy showed me a "hammock tent" on his phone, I laughed.

"That's a gimmick," I told him. "No way that thing holds up in real weather. It's a fancy banana sling with a rainfly taped on top."

I'd been tent camping for twenty years. I had a system. It wasn't comfortable — I'll admit that — but it worked. I knew how to find flat ground, how to avoid puddles, how to layer sleeping pads to dull the rocks digging into my spine.

I was solving a problem that shouldn't exist. But I didn't see that yet.

Then Bob Mackay's review showed up in a camping forum I follow. He'd tested his Hawk Nest in his backyard — 3°C, raining, 15-knot winds. Not the wilderness, but real conditions. And he loved it. That got my attention.

Chapter 2

The Backyard TestWhere I planned to prove everyone wrong

Here's what I expected: I'd set up the Hawk Nest between the two oaks in my backyard, sleep in it for one night, write a scathing review about how tents are superior, and move on with my life.

Setup took me about ten minutes the first time. That surprised me — I expected twenty. Two trees, straps, clip in, spread the bars, toss on the rainfly. Done.

Then I climbed in.

Something was different.

I wasn't curved like a banana. The spreader bar system — this patent-pending design — kept the hammock wide and flat. I could lie on my back. I could lie on my side. I could actually move without feeling like I was going to flip out.

The ground was two feet below me. No rocks. No roots. No cold earth leeching heat from my body. Just... comfort.

I slept seven hours straight. In my backyard. On a Tuesday.

Chapter 3

The Real Test Came NextAnd I was ready this time

The backyard was one thing. But I needed to know if this thing could handle real conditions. So I took it on a weekend trip to a lake where I'd tent-camped dozens of times before.

The campsite that usually gave me trouble — rocky, slightly sloped, always damp — suddenly didn't matter. I strapped up between two pines, and my "campsite" was wherever the trees were. Flat ground? Irrelevant.

That night it rained. I lay there listening to the rain patter on the fly, completely dry, swaying slightly in the breeze, thinking about every miserable wet night I'd spent in a tent with condensation dripping on my face.

I listed my tent on marketplace the following Monday.
